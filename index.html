Nuvation Engineering

In this Linux application, we designed the software architecture in a modular fashion such that interfaces were independently developed for each hardware accessory. Systems/Mechanical Co-op
Synaptive Medical

At Synaptive I was on a team designing a new version of their Drive product, an advanced device for neurosurgery. My role primarily focused on the electronics within the device that supported all functionality visible to the user. My responsibility was to design the cables necessary for distributing power and providing communication lines for all of these components. I first drafted a wiring diagram to show and understand how all parts were connected. Next I completed comprehensive research to select connectors to use at custom interfaces that met all electrical and medical-grade requirements. Finally, I determined the required conductor count, wire gauge, and length to allow me to generate cable drawings using SolidWorks for vendor manufacture. Other tasks I completed at Synaptive included sourcing components for inside Drive and liaising with suppliers to ensure timely and accurate delivery of machined parts.

Mechanical Engineering
Voxel8

I had the opportunity of being with Voxel8 during the exciting start-up phase of bringing their 3D electronics printer to market. I used their prototype units to design and develop novel 3D printed electronics to show the possibilities of the technology. This role included designing prints in SolidWorks and programming an ATtiny84 microcontroller for a 3D printed watch using the Arduino IDE. Along the way, as I became one of the most experienced users of these machines, I identified issues and prototyped solutions. For example I designed several different wiping brushes where the printer could clean its two nozzles before switching between them and I designed a jig for mounting a force gauge and the extruder to characterize the force on the filament at which extrusion failure occurred. In my most significant project of the term, I designed a test fixture in SolidWorks and created an accompanying LabVIEW program to measure the repeatability of the silver nozzle actuation mechanism to fractions of a millimeter with a laser profilometer and automatically store and plot the data it collected over tens of thousands of cycles.

Design Engineering Assistant
Amphenol Canada

I focused primarily on mechanical design and drafting while at Amphenol, designing new connector products and updating and creating production and sales drawings in SolidWorks and AutoCAD. One of my most significant projects was the design of a rugged USB-C receptacle for PCB mounting. This was especially interesting because the connector interface was new from the USB specification so there was much uncharted territory and in particular the size of the contacts made for quite a challenge in design for manufacturing. Another exciting project was the design of a polypropylene injection moulded dust cover with a living hinge. Finally, my most comprehensive project of the term was the design of a fixture for automating the lifecycle testing of a new latching USB receptacle. Actuated only by the vertical motion of an existing machine, the fixture had to simulate normal cycling of the plug insertion, latching, unlatching, and plug removal. Under the supervision of my manager, I was responsible for designing the mechanism in SolidWorks and sourcing all required components. In addition to all of this design work, I also had the opportunity to analyze faulty connectors from production and implement process changes to prevent the issues from resurfacing.

    LED Cube

    To gain some embedded systems experience, I built an LED cube that could be controlled by either an Arduino Uno or a Raspberry Pi. The twenty pins required to control the 4x4x4 cube are concentrated to a single connector which can be driven directly from either the Arduino or Pi or through an intermediate circuit that uses shift registers to reduce the required number of pins on the Arduino or Pi to ten. This project allowed me to better understand digital circuits and get experience working with transistors and shift registers.

    Caliper with Absolute Encoder

    In MTE 201, a statistics course, we were tasked with building and calibrating a sensor. To measure distance, three classmates and I built a caliper with a rack and pinion. The rotation of the pinion was then measured using an absolute encoder made out of photoresistors. The position was captured and read by an Arduino Uno.
